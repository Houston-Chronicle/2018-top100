    To live and dine in Houston

    Back to the main page

    This is the seventh year in which I have produced a Top 100 Restaurants list for the Houston Chronicle.

    Looking back on that first list, from 2012, it's interesting to see how much has changed: 58 of those 100 restaurants — almost 60 percent — are nowhere to be seen on this year's list.

    Fourteen of them have closed. Six have been reconstituted in significantly different form. Some slipped away as the dining scene evolved, competition in their categories heightened, or other categories experienced a sudden, notable boom. The presence of more barbecue and French restaurants this year, for instance, denotes a happy renaissance in those particular cuisines.

    In other cases, a restaurant’s consistent delivery faltered. Yet it’s consistent quality that has kept 42 restaurants on that 2012 lineup in the Top 100 mix today. Consistency is what matters most to me; it even takes precedence over brilliance. My choices stay at the top of their game in their respective class, whether that be high or low, pricey or affordable.

    My picks have never been confined to the fanciest or most expensive restaurants in town; this is not a roster of Houston's finest fine dining. Instead, the Top 100 is meant to be a vivid tapestry of excellence that pulls from all the categories and cuisines that makes this city such an interesting place in which to eat, and to live.

    BEST OF THE BEST: 10 standout dishes from this year's list

    So give a welcome — and a visit — to the 17 newcomers on this year's list. Salute the five returnees who've upped their performance after a hiatus. Give a moment of silence for the six of last year's Top 100 that have closed. And take time to contemplate the long shadow of Hurricane Harvey, which hangs over this year's dining scene like a damp, unwelcome ghost.

    I was struck, as I went about my hundreds of visits and revisits in compiling this list, by the ways in which the hurricane has affected Houston restaurants. Some of the year's high-profile closures are related to the storm, either directly or indirectly. Other restaurants poised to move or renovate have had to delay their plans, given the disruption to the construction industry.

    One restaurant on this year's list, Brasserie 1895, actually changed its business plan substantially — and very successfully — in Harvey's wake, by concentrating on pick-up meals to feed its Friendswood neighborhood as people dried out after the floods. Other restaurant industry folks performed heroically to feed people in the weeks after the storm.

    It's a tribute to all that this year's Top 100 is as exciting a group as it's ever been. Go try them out; you'll see what I mean.

    -Alison Cook

    About Alison Cook

    Alison Cook has been the Houston Chronicle’s restaurant critic since 2002. She has won two James Beard Foundation awards for restaurant criticism, as well as the coveted M.F.K. Fisher Award for Distinguished Writing. Cook’s writing also has appeared in Texas Monthly, Esquire, GQ, The New York Times Magazine, Gourmet, Saveur, Food & Wine, Vogue, Travel & Leisure and House & Garden. Follow her on Twitter (@alisoncook) and read her reviews online at houstonchronicle.com/flavor.
